Sensible Heat
Heat transfer for a temperature change
$$Q = m C_p \Delta T$$
Sensible heat is energy added or removed that causes a temperature change without a phase change. $C_p$ is the specific heat at constant pressure. For liquid water, $C_p \approx 4184\ \text{J/kg·K}$.

Fourier Conduction
Steady-state heat conduction through a flat wall
$$q = \dfrac{k A \,\Delta T}{L}$$
Fourier's law for steady-state 1D conduction through a slab. Thermal resistance: $R_{th} = L/(kA)$. For composite walls in series, resistances add: $R_{total} = \sum L_i/(k_i A)$.
All fields required; L must be nonzero.

Convection (Newton's Law)
Surface heat flux from h, area, and ΔT
$$q = h A \left(T_s - T_\infty\right)$$
$h$ is the convective heat transfer coefficient — depends on geometry, flow, and fluid properties, not a material constant. Free convection in air: $h \approx 2{-}25\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$. Forced liquid convection: $h \approx 500{-}10{,}000\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$.

Nusselt → h
Convection coefficient from Nusselt number
$$h = \dfrac{Nu \cdot k}{L_c}$$
$Nu = hL_c/k$ is the dimensionless heat transfer coefficient. Once $Nu$ is found from a correlation (Dittus-Boelter, Churchill-Bernstein, etc.), this gives $h$. $L_c$ is diameter for pipe flow, length for flat plate.
All fields required; L_c must be nonzero.

Radiation Heat Transfer
Net radiative flux between two surfaces
$$q = \varepsilon \sigma A \left(T_1^4 - T_2^4\right)$$σ = 5.67×10⁻⁸ W/m²·K⁴
Stefan-Boltzmann law for a gray body radiating to a large enclosure. $\varepsilon$ is emissivity (0–1); a blackbody has $\varepsilon = 1$. Temperatures must be in Kelvin. Note the $T^4$ dependence — radiation dominates at high temperatures.
All fields required; ε between 0–1.

LMTD Calculator
Log mean temperature difference
$$\Delta T_{lm} = \dfrac{\Delta T_1 - \Delta T_2}{\ln\!\left(\dfrac{\Delta T_1}{\Delta T_2}\right)}$$
The LMTD is the effective average driving force in a heat exchanger. Counter-current: $\Delta T_1 = T_{h,in} - T_{c,out}$, $\Delta T_2 = T_{h,out} - T_{c,in}$. Counter-current always yields a larger LMTD than parallel flow for the same terminal temperatures.
Temperature differences must be positive and all fields filled.

Heat Exchanger Area
Required area from duty, U, and LMTD
$$A = \dfrac{Q}{U \,\Delta T_{lm}}$$
$U$ is the overall heat transfer coefficient accounting for all resistances in series. Typical values: shell-and-tube liquid–liquid $U \approx 300{-}1000\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$; gas–gas $U \approx 10{-}50\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$.
All fields required; U and ΔTlm must be nonzero.

Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient
U from convection and conduction resistances
$$\frac{1}{UA} = \frac{1}{h_i A} + \frac{t}{kA} + \frac{1}{h_o A}$$
For a flat wall, resistances add in series. This calculator assumes equal inner and outer area (flat plate or thin-wall approximation). For cylindrical walls (pipes), area terms differ and a log-mean area is used instead.

Biot Number
Check lumped capacitance validity
$$Bi = \dfrac{h L_c}{k}$$Lumped valid if Bi < 0.1
$L_c = V/A_s$ is the characteristic length (volume / surface area). If $Bi < 0.1$, internal conduction resistance is negligible and the object can be treated as spatially uniform (lumped capacitance model). $L_c$ for a sphere: $r/3$; for a long cylinder: $r/2$; for a plane wall: $L/2$.
All fields required; k must be nonzero.

Lumped Capacitance Temperature
Transient temperature decay (Bi < 0.1)
$$T(t) = T_\infty + (T_i - T_\infty)\, e^{-t/\tau}$$τ = ρ V Cp / (h As)
Valid only when $Bi = hL_c/k < 0.1$. The time constant $\tau = \rho V C_p / (h A_s)$ governs the exponential decay. At $t = \tau$, the object has reached $\approx 63\%$ of its final temperature change.
All fields required; τ must be nonzero.

Mass Balance
Steady-state single-component balance
$$\dot{m}_{accum} = \dot{m}_{in} - \dot{m}_{out}$$
At steady state, accumulation = 0, so $\dot{m}_{in} = \dot{m}_{out}$. A nonzero result means the system mass is changing — valid for batch or transient problems.

Mixing Concentration
Outlet concentration for two streams
$$C_{out} = \dfrac{F_1 C_1 + F_2 C_2}{F_1 + F_2}$$
Component material balance on a mixing tee assuming complete mixing and no reaction. $C$ can be any consistent concentration unit. Extend to $n$ streams by summing numerator and denominator.

Open System Energy Balance
Steady-flow heat duty
$$\dot{Q} = \dot{m}\, C_p\, \Delta T$$
Simplified steady-state energy balance neglecting kinetic/potential energy and shaft work. Valid for most HX and reactor sizing. For phase-change processes include latent heat: $\dot{Q} = \dot{m}(C_p \Delta T + \Delta H_{vap})$.

Reynolds Number
Determine laminar or turbulent flow regime
$$Re = \dfrac{\rho v D}{\mu}$$
Ratio of inertial to viscous forces. Pipe flow: $Re < 2300$ laminar, $2300{-}4000$ transitional, $Re > 4000$ turbulent. For flat plate, critical $Re \approx 5 \times 10^5$.
All fields required; μ must be nonzero.

Darcy–Weisbach ΔP
Frictional pressure drop in a pipe
$$\Delta P = f_D\,\dfrac{L}{D}\,\dfrac{\rho v^2}{2}$$
$f_D$ is the Darcy friction factor. Laminar ($Re < 2300$): $f_D = 64/Re$. Turbulent: use Colebrook or Swamee-Jain (see below). Note: Fanning friction factor $f = f_D/4$.
All fields required; D must be nonzero.

Friction Factor (Swamee-Jain)
Explicit approximation of Colebrook equation
$$f_D = \dfrac{0.25}{\left[\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{\varepsilon/D}{3.7} + \dfrac{5.74}{Re^{0.9}}\right)\right]^2}$$
Explicit approximation of the implicit Colebrook equation, accurate to ~3% for $10^{-6} \le \varepsilon/D \le 10^{-2}$ and $5000 \le Re \le 10^8$. For laminar flow ($Re < 2300$): $f_D = 64/Re$.
All fields required; D must be nonzero.

Equations of State
💨
Ideal Gas Law
Solve for pressure using PV = nRT
$$P = \dfrac{nRT}{V}$$R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹
Valid at low pressure and high temperature relative to the critical point. For non-ideal behavior: $PV = ZnRT$ where $Z$ is the compressibility factor. Common correlations: Pitzer, van der Waals, Peng-Robinson.
All fields required; V must be nonzero.
Pressure
—Pa
🔵
Van der Waals EOS
Real gas pressure from T and V
$$P = \dfrac{nRT}{V - nb} - a\dfrac{n^2}{V^2}$$
$a$ corrects for intermolecular attraction; $b$ corrects for finite molecular volume. Constants are species-specific (e.g., for CO₂: $a = 0.3640\ \text{Pa·m}^6/\text{mol}^2$, $b = 4.267\times10^{-5}\ \text{m}^3/\text{mol}$). For an ideal gas $a=b=0$.
All fields required; (V − nb) must be positive.

Antoine Vapor Pressure
Saturation pressure from temperature
$$\log_{10} P^{sat} = A - \dfrac{B}{C + T}$$
Empirical correlation using species-specific constants A, B, C. Constants are unit-specific — always verify (T in °C or K, $P^{sat}$ in mmHg, bar, or kPa). Defaults below are for water with T in °C and $P^{sat}$ in mmHg (valid 60–150 °C).
All fields required.

Clausius-Clapeyron
Vapor pressure at a new temperature
$$\ln\!\frac{P_2}{P_1} = -\frac{\Delta H_{vap}}{R}\!\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right)$$
Assumes $\Delta H_{vap}$ is constant over the temperature range and the vapor behaves ideally. Most accurate for modest temperature differences. $R = 8.314\ \text{J/mol·K}$; $T$ must be in Kelvin; $P$ units cancel so any consistent pressure unit works.
All fields required; T₁ and T₂ must be nonzero.

Carnot Efficiency
Maximum possible thermal efficiency
$$\eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \dfrac{T_C}{T_H}$$
The Carnot efficiency is the theoretical maximum for any heat engine operating between $T_H$ and $T_C$. No real engine can exceed it. Both temperatures must be in Kelvin. Real cycles (Rankine, Brayton) achieve 30–45% of this limit.
Both temperatures required; T_H must be greater than T_C and nonzero.

COP — Refrigerator / Heat Pump
Coefficient of performance for cooling or heating
$$COP_{ref} = \dfrac{T_C}{T_H - T_C} \qquad COP_{hp} = \dfrac{T_H}{T_H - T_C}$$
COP is the ratio of desired output (cooling or heating) to work input. These are Carnot (maximum) COPs. Real systems achieve 40–60% of these values. Note $COP_{hp} = COP_{ref} + 1$ always.
Both temperatures required; T_H must be greater than T_C.

Isentropic Compression / Expansion
Outlet temperature and work for adiabatic process
$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)^{\!\frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma}}$$
For an isentropic (reversible adiabatic) ideal gas process. $\gamma = C_p/C_v$: for diatomic gases (N₂, O₂, air) $\gamma \approx 1.4$; monatomic (Ar, He) $\gamma \approx 1.67$; polyatomic $\gamma \approx 1.1{-}1.3$. Shaft work: $W_s = \dot{m} C_p (T_2 - T_1)$.
All fields required; P₁ and γ must be nonzero.

Reactor Conversion
Fractional conversion from molar flow rates
$$X = \dfrac{F_{A0} - F_A}{F_{A0}}$$
Conversion $X$ is defined on the limiting reactant A. For batch reactor, replace flow rates with moles: $X = (N_{A0} - N_A)/N_{A0}$. Conversion is always between 0 and 1.
All fields required; F_A0 must be nonzero.

CSTR Volume
Required volume for a given conversion
$$V = \dfrac{F_{A0}\, X}{-r_A}$$
Perfect mixing assumed — exit conditions equal interior conditions. Rate $-r_A$ is evaluated at exit conversion $X$. For first-order: $-r_A = k C_{A0}(1-X)$. PFR always outperforms a single CSTR for positive-order reactions.
All fields required; -r_A must be nonzero and X between 0–1.
Reactor Volume V
—m³
🔬
PFR Volume
Levenspiel integral for first-order reaction
$$V = \dfrac{F_{A0}}{k\, C_{A0}} \ln\!\left(\dfrac{1}{1-X}\right)$$
PFR design equation $V = F_{A0}\int_0^X dX/(-r_A)$ integrated for an irreversible first-order liquid-phase reaction where $-r_A = k C_{A0}(1-X)$. PFR always outperforms a single CSTR for positive-order reactions.
All fields required; k, C_A0 must be nonzero and X between 0–0.9999.

⏳
Space Time τ
Reactor residence time from volume and flow
$$\tau = \dfrac{V}{v_0} = \dfrac{C_{A0} V}{F_{A0}}$$
Space time $\tau$ is the time needed to process one reactor volume of feed at inlet conditions. It equals mean residence time only for constant-density (liquid-phase) systems. The Damköhler number $Da = \tau k$ (1st order) indicates conversion level: $Da \gg 1$ → high $X$.
All fields required; F_A0 must be nonzero.

Kinetics

Arrhenius Rate Constant
k₂ from k₁, Eₐ, T₁, and T₂
$$k_2 = k_1 \exp\!\left[-\frac{E_a}{R}\!\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right)\right]$$
Derived from $k = A e^{-E_a/RT}$ by taking the ratio at two temperatures. $E_a$ in J/mol, $R = 8.314\ \text{J/mol·K}$. A 10 °C rise roughly doubles $k$ for typical $E_a \approx 50{-}80\ \text{kJ/mol}$.
All fields required; T₁ and T₂ must be nonzero.
Rate Constant k₂
—same units as k₁

Reaction Half-Life
t₁/₂ for 1st and 2nd order reactions
$$t_{1/2}^{(1)} = \dfrac{\ln 2}{k} \qquad t_{1/2}^{(2)} = \dfrac{1}{k C_{A0}}$$
For 1st order, half-life is independent of concentration. For 2nd order, it depends on initial concentration. 0th order: $t_{1/2} = C_{A0}/(2k)$. These apply to irreversible liquid-phase reactions.
All fields required; k and C_A0 must be nonzero.

Michaelis-Menten Kinetics
Enzyme reaction rate
$$-r_A = \dfrac{V_{max}\, C_A}{K_M + C_A}$$
$V_{max}$ is the maximum reaction rate (at saturating substrate). $K_M$ is the Michaelis constant — the substrate concentration at half-maximum rate. When $C_A \ll K_M$, the kinetics are pseudo-first-order; when $C_A \gg K_M$, the reaction is zeroth-order.
All fields required; K_M + C_A must be nonzero.
Reaction Rate -r_A
—mol/m³·s
🔢
Damköhler Number
Dimensionless reaction rate vs. transport rate
$$Da = \tau \cdot k \quad \text{(1st order)}$$
$Da$ compares reaction rate to flow rate. $Da \ll 1$: reaction is slow relative to flow, low conversion. $Da \gg 1$: reaction is fast, high conversion. For a CSTR: $X = Da/(1+Da)$ for first-order kinetics.
Both fields required.

Batch Reactor Integrated Rate Laws
0️⃣
0th Order Batch
Concentration vs. time for zero-order reaction
$$C_A = C_{A0} - k\, t$$
Zero-order: rate is independent of concentration ($-r_A = k$). Valid at high substrate concentrations or surface-catalyzed reactions at saturation. $C_A$ cannot go negative — reaction stops when $C_A = 0$ at $t = C_{A0}/k$.
All fields required.
Concentration C_A
—mol/m³
1️⃣
1st Order Batch
Concentration vs. time for first-order reaction
$$C_A = C_{A0}\, e^{-kt}$$
Most common kinetic form. Rate $-r_A = kC_A$ — proportional to concentration. Radioactive decay, many homogeneous reactions. Half-life $t_{1/2} = \ln2/k$ is constant and independent of initial concentration.
All fields required.
Concentration C_A
—mol/m³
2️⃣
2nd Order Batch
Concentration vs. time for second-order reaction
$$\dfrac{1}{C_A} = \dfrac{1}{C_{A0}} + k\, t$$
For an irreversible bimolecular reaction $A + A \to P$ with $-r_A = k C_A^2$. A plot of $1/C_A$ vs. $t$ is linear with slope $k$. Half-life depends on initial concentration: $t_{1/2} = 1/(kC_{A0})$.
All fields required; denominator must be positive.

Fick's Law (Diffusion Flux)
Molar flux from concentration gradient
$$J_A = -D_{AB}\,\dfrac{dC_A}{dz}$$
Fick's first law for steady-state equimolar counter-diffusion. Finite slab form: $J_A = D_{AB}(C_{A1}-C_{A2})/\Delta z$. Typical $D_{AB}$: gases $\sim10^{-5}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$, liquids $\sim10^{-9}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$.
All fields required; Δz must be nonzero.
Molar Flux J_A

Convective Mass Transfer
Flux from mass transfer coefficient k_c
$$N_A = k_c \left(C_{A,s} - C_{A,\infty}\right)$$
Analogous to Newton's law of cooling. $k_c$ (m/s) is obtained from Sherwood number correlations. Chilton-Colburn analogy: $k_c = h/(\rho C_p \cdot Le^{2/3})$.

Sherwood Number → k_c
Mass transfer coefficient from Sh
$$k_c = \dfrac{Sh \cdot D_{AB}}{L_c}$$
$Sh = k_c L_c / D_{AB}$ is the mass transfer analog of the Nusselt number. Turbulent pipe correlation: $Sh = 0.023 Re^{0.8} Sc^{1/3}$. $Sc = \mu/(\rho D_{AB})$ is the Schmidt number.
All fields required; L_c must be nonzero.

—m/s
📊
Schmidt Number
Ratio of momentum to mass diffusivity
$$Sc = \dfrac{\mu}{\rho\, D_{AB}} = \dfrac{\nu}{D_{AB}}$$
$Sc \ll 1$: liquid metals. $Sc \approx 1$: gases. $Sc \gg 1$: liquids. Mass transfer analog of Prandtl number in heat transfer. Appears in Sh correlations.
All fields required; ρ and D_AB must be nonzero.
